Antiderivatives and the Definite Integral

Definition 1 (Antiderivative)

A function $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$ if $F'(x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$.

Definition 2 (Riemann Integral)

For $f$ bounded on $[a,b]$, the definite integral $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is the limit of Riemann sums, when it exists.

  • Differentiation measures instantaneous rate of change.
  • Integration measures accumulated quantity (area).
  • The FTC reveals these are inverse operations.

The First Fundamental Theorem

Theorem 1 (FTC, Part I)

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a,b]$. Define $\displaystyle F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$. Then $F$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and

$$F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in (a,b).$$

Proof sketch.

By definition, $\displaystyle F'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt$. Since $f$ is continuous, the Mean Value Theorem for integrals gives $\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = f(c_h)$ for some $c_h$ between $x$ and $x+h$. As $h \to 0$, we have $c_h \to x$, so $F'(x) = f(x)$.

The Second Fundamental Theorem

Theorem 2 (FTC, Part II — Evaluation Theorem)

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a,b]$ and let $F$ be any antiderivative of $f$. Then

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).$$

  • Reduces computing integrals to finding antiderivatives.
  • We write $F(b) - F(a) = \bigl[F(x)\bigr]_a^b$ for brevity.
  • Any two antiderivatives differ by a constant, so the choice of $F$ does not matter.

Applying the FTC

Example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^3 (2x + 1)\,dx$.

Solution.

An antiderivative is $F(x) = x^2 + x$. By the FTC:

$$\int_1^3 (2x+1)\,dx = \bigl[x^2 + x\bigr]_1^3 = (9+3) - (1+1) = 10.$$
